Email Templates

Ready-to-use language for contacting production accounting about missing or incorrect pay. Always attach your Exhibit G, paystub, and contract.

Every email must: (1) clearly state in the first sentence what is missing or wrong, (2) include all relevant attachments, and (3) be forwardable as-is — production may pass it to their payroll service.
Stunt Adjustment Not Paid
Subject: Missing Stunt Adjustment Payment — [Your Name] — [Show Title] — [Work Date] Hi [Coordinator/Payroll Contact Name], My stunt adjustment was not paid for my work on [Show Title] on [Date]. Per my contract and Exhibit G, I am owed a stunt adjustment of $[Amount] for [description of stunt/gag]. This does not appear on my paystub. Please advise on when a corrected check can be issued. Attached: Exhibit G, paystub, contract Thank you, [Your Name]
Stunt Adjustment Excluded from OT Calculation
Subject: Overtime Underpayment — Stunt Adjustment Not Included in OT Base — [Your Name] — [Show Title] — [Work Date] Hi [Coordinator/Payroll Contact Name], My overtime was calculated incorrectly on [Show Title] on [Date]. My stunt adjustment of $[Amount] was not factored into the base rate used to calculate overtime. Per SAG-AFTRA rules, the stunt adjustment must be included in the base rate before overtime hourly rates are calculated. Correct OT base: $[Scale Rate] + $[Adjustment] = $[Total]. OT hourly (1.5x): $[Correct Rate]. I was paid at $[Incorrect Rate]. Please issue a corrected check for the difference. Attached: Exhibit G, paystub, contract Thank you, [Your Name]
Meal Penalties Not Paid
Subject: Missing Meal Penalty Payment — [Your Name] — [Show Title] — [Work Date] Hi [Coordinator/Payroll Contact Name], Meal penalties were not paid for my work on [Show Title] on [Date]. Per my Exhibit G, my meal break was delayed beyond the 6-hour threshold. Meal penalties should have accrued from [Time] until my meal break at [Time], totaling approximately $[Amount]. Please issue a corrected payment. Attached: Exhibit G, paystub, contract Thank you, [Your Name]
Late Payment
Subject: Late Payment — [Your Name] — [Show Title] — [Work Date(s)] Hi [Coordinator/Payroll Contact Name], I have not received payment for my work on [Show Title] on [Date(s)]. Per SAG-AFTRA rules, this check is now late. Late payment penalties of $10/day (excluding Sat/Sun/holidays) are accruing up to a maximum of $200. Please advise on the status of my payment and issue any applicable late payment penalties along with the outstanding amount. Payroll service: [EP / Cast & Crew / GreenSlate / Other] Attached: Exhibit G, contract Thank you, [Your Name]

Paystub Errors

The most common mistakes production accounting makes — and the language to use when disputing them.

Always attach your Exhibit G, paystub, and contract when contacting production about an error. The first sentence of any email should clearly state what is missing or incorrect.
01

Stunt Adjustment Not Paid

You performed a stunt that qualifies for an adjustment, but it doesn't appear on your paystub at all.

02

Meal Penalties Not Paid

Your meal break was delayed beyond the allowed time but no penalty payment was added.

03

Overtime Not Paid

You worked beyond the straight-time hours but overtime pay was not included.

04

Travel Time Not Paid

Time spent traveling to/from a distant location is compensable work time — but often missing.

05

Check Never Received

Payment was due but you never received a check or direct deposit.

06

Base Rate Incorrect

Your daily or weekly rate was set below SAG-AFTRA scale minimums.

07

Stunt Adjustment Excluded from OT Base

Your stunt adjustment must be factored into the base rate used to calculate overtime — if it isn't, all OT hourly rates are wrong.

08

Meal Penalty Miscalculated

A meal penalty was paid but the amount is incorrect.

09

Location Allowance Not Paid

On a weekly contract at an overnight location, you are owed an additional 4 hours of straight time — whether or not all 6 days were worked. This is frequently missing.

10

Fitting Not Paid

You attended a wardrobe fitting and received no compensation.

11

Fitting Underpaid

Fitting pay was included but calculated incorrectly.

12

Check Is Late

Your check is past the contractual payment deadline — late penalties may be owed.

13

Holiday Pay Missing/Miscalculated

Holiday pay was not paid or was computed incorrectly.

14

Reuse Pay Miscalculated

Your footage was reused (recap, clip, etc.) but the reuse payment is wrong.

15

Individual vs. Corp Payment Error

You were paid as an individual when you should have been paid through your loan-out corporation (or vice versa).

Overtime Rules

Overtime is one of the most frequently underpaid items. How it's calculated depends on your contract type.

When Am I in Overtime?

Select your contract type to see when overtime kicks in.

Pay is computed from time of first call to dismissal, excluding meal periods. OT is calculated in 1/10th hourly units (6-minute increments).

  • Hours 1–8: Straight time
  • Hours 9–10: 1.5x your hourly rate
  • Hours 11+: 2.0x your hourly rate

Money break (Theatrical & TV Day Performers): 2x the minimum day performer rate. Performers earning over the money break receive 1.5x after 10 hours, based on the money break amount.

Your hourly rate = daily rate / 8. If you have a stunt adjustment, it must be included in that base before dividing.

Source: SAG-AFTRA Overtime (Theatrical)

The 3-day contract applies to Television only. Two types of overtime can apply:

  • Daily OT: 2x beyond 10 hours per day
  • Cumulative OT: 1.5x beyond 24 cumulative straight-time hours over the 3-day period (or beyond 32 hours over 4 days if guaranteed)
  • Days beyond guarantee: OT begins after 8 hours — 1.5x for hours 9–10, 2x after that

Money break: $3,000.

Source: SAG-AFTRA Overtime (Theatrical & Television)

Schedule B (guaranteed $6,200/week or less):

  • Daily OT: 2x beyond 10 hours per day
  • Weekly OT: 1.5x beyond 44 hours (studio) or 48 hours (overnight location)

Schedule C (guaranteed more than $6,200/week):

  • Daily OT: 2x beyond 10 hours per day, on a maximum basis of $5,500

Money breaks: TV weekly = $4,400  |  Theatrical weekly = $5,500

Source: SAG-AFTRA Overtime (Theatrical & Television)
  • Contract rate must be stated as the 5-day studio week rate — even if you're on location
  • The 6th day automatically triggers 4 hours of overtime at straight-time rates
  • All overtime is computed on the 5-day studio week rate
  • Schedule B weekly OT threshold increases from 44 to 48 hours on overnight location workweeks

The contract must show the 5-day rate even if location shooting is involved — a common source of accounting errors.

  • Schedule K — Stunt Coordinators (Flat Rate): No overtime. Flat deal coordinators receive premium pay or penalty pay instead. Includes on-camera work.
  • Schedule K — Non-Flat Rate: Paid per Schedule K Part 1 or Part 2.

Workweek Rules

The SAG-AFTRA workweek structure determines your 6th and 7th day premiums and triggers the location allowance on overnight locations.

Quick Reference
Workweek TypeWork Days6th Day7th DayLocation Allowance
StudioAny 5 of 71.5x2x—
Overnight Location (Weekly)Any 6 of 71.5x2x+4 hrs straight time
Studio Workweek

The studio workweek consists of any 5 days out of 7 consecutive days, with the 6th and 7th days as regular days off. The Producer designates which days are work days.

Premium rates when a performer works the 6th or 7th day:

  • 6th day: 1.5x
  • 7th day: 2x

Note: There are exceptions to 6th day premium rules under SAG-AFTRA Low Budget theatrical agreements.

Source: SAG-AFTRA Workweek
Shifting the Workweek

The production workweek may be shifted without penalty in two ways:

  • By adding one or two days off consecutive with the 6th and/or 7th days off in the prior workweek
  • By shifting from a workweek starting on a Tuesday to one starting on a Monday, provided the intervening Sunday is a day off

If production changes your workweek in a way that costs you 6th or 7th day premium pay, verify that the shift was done properly under these rules.

Overnight Location Workweek & Location Allowance

The overnight location workweek consists of any 6 days out of 7 consecutive days.

The 6-day overnight location workweek — whether those 6 days are worked or not — automatically triggers an additional 4 hours of straight-time salary for weekly performers. This is the "location allowance."

The location allowance is one of the most commonly missed payments on distant location shoots. If you were on a weekly contract at an overnight location and don't see an extra 4 hours of straight time on your Exhibit G, it was likely not paid.
Source: SAG-AFTRA Workweek

Meal Penalties

SAG-AFTRA rules require that you receive a meal break within 6 hours. If that window is missed, you're owed a meal penalty.

Meal Penalty Calculator
Meal Penalty Owed
$0.00
The Basic Rule

You must receive a meal break no later than 6 hours after your call time (or after your previous meal break). If production goes past that point, a meal penalty begins accruing.

Meal Penalty Amounts — SAG-AFTRA Principal Performers
  • First ½ hour (or portion): $25.00
  • Second ½ hour (or portion): $35.00
  • Each succeeding ½ hour (or portion): $50.00

Source: 2024 Jeffords Rules — SAG-AFTRA Meal Periods

Non-Deductible (ND) Meal Rule

Any meal provided within 2 hours of your call time (at least 15 minutes, free of all activity, appropriate to time of day) is a non-deductible (ND) meal. An ND meal does NOT count as your meal break, but it resets the 6-hour clock — the meal window runs from the time the ND meal was served.

Common Errors with Meal Penalties
  • Not paid at all — production missed the meal window but no penalty appears on your check
  • Wrong amount — the penalty amount per increment was calculated incorrectly
  • "Grace period" misuse — a 12-minute unplanned grace period is available if the 6-hour window hasn't been extended, but it cannot be scheduled or made automatic
  • Travel time question — since travel time counts as work time, if travel pushes you past 6 hours without a meal, a penalty may be owed (check with SAG-AFTRA)

Travel Time & Location Expenses

Travel time to and from a distant location is treated as work time. Per diem and flight class rules are set by SAG-AFTRA contract.

Travel Time = Work Time

When you travel to or from a distant location (outside the studio zone requiring an overnight stay), that travel time counts as work time. It must appear on your Exhibit G — including triggering overtime if your total hours exceed thresholds. Travel-related overtime is capped at 1.5x maximum.

Per Diem & Accommodations

On overnight locations, the Producer must provide a per diem meal allowance, paid prior to the day or week of work. Per diem is only owed if meals are not provided by the Producer. Minimum amounts:

  • Breakfast: $12.00
  • Lunch: $18.00
  • Dinner: $30.00
  • Total: $60.00/day minimum

Meal deduction rule: Regardless of call time, the first major meal served may only be deducted at the lunch rate ($18).

The 2024 Jeffords Rules reference $70/day (effective 11/9/23) and $75/day (effective 11/9/25) — reflecting negotiated increases above the contractual minimum. Verify current rate with your payroll company or SAG-AFTRA.

When on overnight location for two weeks or more, a check cashing facility must be available if per diem is paid by check.

Source: SAG-AFTRA Transportation and Location Expenses
Flight Class Rules

All flights shall be business class when available, or first class if business is not available.

Coach class is acceptable only when:

  • Domestic non-stop flights under 1,000 air miles
  • Non-stop flights between LA and Vancouver
  • Non-stop flights under 1,000 miles between the US and Vancouver or Toronto
  • A substantial number of the company is being transported
  • 6 or more performers travel together on the same flight
  • Auditions and interviews

If you fly coach: Producer must provide elevated coach (Economy Plus, extended leg room) when available. You must be reimbursed for baggage fees and in-flight meals — submit receipts within 30 days.

Source: SAG-AFTRA Transportation and Location Expenses
Local vs. Distant vs. Regional Hire
  • Local Hire: No travel expenses or per diem owed
  • Regional Hire (Modified Local): Mileage (not airfare) covered; sometimes lodging and per diem
  • Distant Location: Full travel time as work time, per diem, lodging, and transportation all apply
Travel time is one of the most frequently omitted pay items. If you traveled to a distant location and don't see travel time on your Exhibit G, it was almost certainly not paid.

Fittings

Wardrobe fittings have specific payment rules that are often overlooked by production.

Wardrobe Fittings — Schedule A (Principal Performers)

For a fitting on a day prior to your work day: minimum 1 hour pay; additional time in 15-minute increments.

For a fitting on the same day as your work call: fitting time is counted as part of your work time.

Important exception: Day performers with a pay rate of $1,500 or more per day are not entitled to pay for a fitting on a day prior to work.

If a wardrobe fitting or HMU test is paid but you are not ultimately called to work that day, you receive one day's pay.

→ Source: 2024 Jeffords Rules — SAG-AFTRA Fittings & Rehearsals

Studio Zones

Check if your basecamp or shooting location falls within a studio zone. Studio zone determines travel pay, per diem, and overnight location rules. The 30-mile radius zones below apply to principal performers and stunt performers.

Zone Map — New York (30-mile radius from Columbus Circle)
Loading map...
Why Studio Zones Matter

If your shooting location is inside the studio zone, production is not required to provide travel pay, per diem, or overnight accommodations. You're expected to get yourself to and from set.

If your location is outside the studio zone and requires an overnight stay, it becomes an overnight location — and production must provide travel pay, per diem, lodging, and your travel time counts as work time.

The zone also affects rest period rules (10-hour reduced rest is only available at certain outside-zone locations) and workweek rules (overnight locations use a 6-day workweek instead of 5).

New York Studio Zone

Any location within a 30-mile radius of Columbus Circle, other than Sandy Hook, New Jersey. This is the zone that applies to principal performers and stunt performers.

Los Angeles Studio Zone

All territory within a 30-mile radius from the intersection of Beverly Boulevard and La Cienega Boulevard, Los Angeles, California. Also includes Agua Dulce, Castaic (including Lake Castaic), Leo Carillo State Beach, Moorpark, Ontario International Airport, Piru and Pomona (including the Los Angeles County Fairgrounds) and other territory generally recognized as being within the zone.

LA Secondary Studio Zone — Why LA Has Two

LA is the only city with a secondary zone — a ring extending 10 miles beyond the perimeter of the primary 30-mile zone, including John Wayne Airport.

The secondary zone exists because LA-area productions frequently shoot at locations that are just outside the 30-mile radius. Rather than triggering full overnight location rules for these nearby locations, the secondary zone creates a middle ground: production may still owe mileage or other allowances, but the full overnight location package (per diem, lodging, travel time as work time) does not automatically apply.

Waivers are available for locations like Lake Hughes, Elizabeth Lake, and the Nikken Building in Irvine to be treated as within the secondary zone.

San Francisco Studio Zone

All territory within a 30-mile radius from the intersection of Market and Powell Streets.

Other Studio Zones

The Union and Producers may agree to recognize other studio zones on a similar basis. In default of agreement, the territory shall be determined by arbitration under Section 50.

Late Payments

Production is required to pay on time. If they're late, penalties accrue — and you may be owed one penalty per missed payroll period, not just a one-time amount.

Late Payment Penalty Calculator
Penalty Owed
$0.00
The Late Payment Penalty

SAG-AFTRA's standard late payment penalty is $10 per day (excluding Saturdays, Sundays, and holidays) up to a maximum of $200. The penalty is calculated from the date production is informed that the check is late and they agree the check is late — not from the original due date. Additional penalties may accrue if a formal written claim is filed through SAG-AFTRA.

→ SAG-AFTRA CBA
One Penalty Per Payroll Period

According to SAG's National Director of Entertainment, a performer should receive a separate late penalty for each payroll period that was paid late or incorrectly. If you were underpaid for three separate weeks, you may be owed three separate late payment penalties.

How Long Do You Have to File?

Principal performers have 6 months from the date the issue occurred to file a grievance. Do not delay.

→ SAG-AFTRA Grievance Filing Reference
Reuse Pay — Late Payment Penalty

Production has 60 business days from the exhibition date to issue reuse payment. If they miss this window, they owe an additional amount equal to the day performer minimum on top of the reuse payment.

→ SAG-AFTRA TV Agreement
The 6-month grievance window starts from when the error occurred — not when you discovered it. Track your pay dates carefully.

Residuals

Residuals are payments owed to performers when their work is reused, rerun, or distributed on additional platforms. They are one of the most valuable — and most misunderstood — parts of SAG-AFTRA compensation.

The Basic Residual Rules
TV: Residuals are owed every time an episode reruns, is licensed to another network, moves to streaming, or is distributed on home video or internationally. Network reruns follow a step-down schedule. Cable and streaming residuals are based on the distributor's gross revenue or the platform's subscriber count.
Movies: Residuals are owed when a theatrical film is exhibited beyond its initial domestic theatrical release — including foreign theatrical, TV broadcast, streaming, and home video. Each window generates separate residuals based on the distributor's gross receipts.
Reuse: If footage from your performance is used in a different context — a recap, clip show, or another production — that's a reuse fee, separate from residuals. Both can be owed.
Timeline: Production has 60 business days from the exhibition date to pay. After that, late penalties apply at $10/day up to $200.
Who pays: Residuals are processed and distributed by SAG-AFTRA based on reports from distributors — not directly from the production company. Commercial residuals are the exception: they are mailed directly by payroll companies.
What Are Residuals?

Residuals are additional compensation paid to performers when a production is exhibited beyond its initial use. Your original session fee covers the first airing or exhibition window. Every subsequent use — reruns, streaming, home video, foreign distribution — triggers a residual payment.

How residuals are calculated depends on the media type. Network TV reruns use a step-down schedule based on the applicable minimum. Most other platforms — cable, streaming, theatrical, home video — base residuals on the distributor's gross revenue, not your session fee. Your individual share is proportional to your session fee relative to total cast compensation.

Types of Residuals
Network TV Reruns — Each rerun triggers a residual payment. The first rerun pays the highest percentage, declining with subsequent airings. Typically starts around 100% of the applicable minimum for the first rerun, stepping down from there.
Basic Cable — Residuals are based on a percentage of the distributor's gross revenue, paid quarterly or semi-annually. The performer's share is calculated based on their original session fee relative to the total cast compensation.
Pay TV / Premium Cable — Similar to basic cable but with different revenue percentages. Covers channels like HBO, Showtime, etc.
Streaming / New Media (SVOD) — Under the 2023 contract, streaming residuals are based on the number of hours of content exhibited on the platform and the platform's subscriber count. This replaced the old "fixed residual" model for most SVOD content.
Theatrical (Domestic) — Feature films exhibited in domestic theaters. Residuals are based on a percentage of distributor's gross receipts.
Theatrical (Foreign) — Feature films exhibited in foreign markets. Separate residual calculations apply based on foreign distributor's gross.
Home Video / DVD — A fixed percentage of the distributor's gross for home video/DVD sales and rentals.
Free TV (Syndication) — When a show is sold to local stations or syndication, residuals are based on distributor's gross.
Reuse vs. Residuals

Reuse is when footage from one production is used in another context — for example, a clip from Episode 5 appearing in a recap at the top of Episode 10. Reuse triggers a separate payment.

Residuals are payments for the same production being exhibited again — reruns, streaming releases, foreign distribution, etc.

Both are owed. They are not the same thing. A production can owe you both reuse fees and residuals.

When Are Residuals Owed?
  • A TV episode reruns on the same network or is sold to another
  • A show moves from broadcast to a streaming platform
  • A film is released on home video, VOD, or streaming after theatrical
  • A production is exhibited in foreign markets
  • Footage is reused in a recap, clip show, or other production

Production has 60 business days from the exhibition date to issue reuse/residual payment. After that, late penalties apply.

The $100 Reuse Fee
reuse fee SAG business rep late-night TV

I once worked on a weekly late-night show where I performed an acrobatic stunt. Later, they reused an image of me from that performance in a recap segment — nothing elaborate, just a still shot of me mid-act. When I realized they hadn't sent any payment for that reuse, it didn't sit right with me. From my understanding, even using a single image like that should qualify for a reuse fee.

So I reached out to SAG and spoke with a business rep, who then contacted the production on my behalf. Eventually, they came back with a payment of $100. Not a full day rate — just $100.

Honestly, it didn't feel right. It wasn't just about the money, though that amount felt low — it was also the principle. They had reused my work without proper compensation, and the resolution didn't reflect that. On top of that, I couldn't shake the feeling that by filing the claim, I might have strained my relationship with that production.

What made it worse was how it was handled. The business rep treated the $100 like it was a big win, but to me, it felt like the production got off easy for breaking a rule. In the end, the whole situation left a bad taste in my mouth. It felt like more trouble than it was worth — and like I hadn't really been backed up the way I should have been.

— James Northrup

Dressing Rooms for Stunt Coordinators
dressing room stunt coordinator studio location

August 15, 2022

Q: Is production required to provide a cast room for the Stunt Coordinator when they are in a studio location?

SAG-AFTRA: In general, when in the studio, production should be providing dressing rooms to all of our members, coordinators included. If there are any limitations on the number of rooms available, we would expect production to reach out and have a discussion with us about such situation. Cross-Boarding: Pay Per Episode for Stunt Coordinators
cross-boarding stunt coordinator episode pay

October 17, 2023

Cross-boarding: When a production films scenes from two or more episodes on the same shooting day.

Q: A coordinator is being carried on a show (working on every episode, every week) and the production is cross-boarding (filming 2 episodes a day). Should the coordinator be paid for two episodes when they cross-board for a day or only get paid for one episode?

SAG-AFTRA: This has been a point of contention with the industry currently but it is the Union's position that stunt coordinators should be given a contract and paid for each episode they render services in. Even if that is on the same day. So, in the case you outline, yes, such a coordinator should be paid per episode they are rendering services on.

Note: "The Union's position" means that studios may have a different interpretation. This is an area of active contention.
Per Diem: Cash, Taxes, and Contract Language
per diem cash payment taxes overnight location

January 30, 2024

Q: Does production have to pay per diem in cash? Does production have to pay it as non-salary income? Does anything change if the production is in Canada? Where is this information located in the contract?

SAG-AFTRA: Let me start on the places in the contract where you can find info on per diem. In the CBA, it is under the ADDITIONAL PROVISIONS on page 105. In the TV Agreement, it is under Section 65 under OVERNIGHT LOCATIONS on page 179 of the TV Agreement.

That said, the provisions do not specifically state that the per diem needs to be paid in cash but the language also states that if a check is issued for the per diem, the producer needs to make certain that an accessible banking location, near to the overnight location where the production is shooting, be available to our members to cash such a check.

As to the timing of the per diem payments, it does state that the per diem payments should be issued "to day performers before the start of each workday and to weekly performers before the start of each workweek." This language is found in the CBA but it is not specifically in the TV Agreement language. However, we take the position that it is due under the TV language as it calls for in the CBA.

However, even with full knowledge of this language, many producers are putting per diem in payroll checks. We are trying to push back on those when the issue arises but one thing to really watch for is when they list the per diem income and have taxes deducted from it. They should not be doing this as it is not taxable income and should not be treated as such.

Promos vs. Recaps: When Is Reuse Pay Owed?
reuse fee promo recap preview residuals

May 18, 2022

Q: A performer was hired to work in episode 18 and his performance was used in the preview/promo after episode 17. Should he be paid a reuse fee if he's featured in a preview/promo that airs after an episode he was not hired to work on? Are there clear contract definitions for "promo," "preview," and "recap" and would the difference in these definitions determine whether or not a performer is paid a reuse fee and residuals?

SAG-AFTRA: While the contract does not define these terms (it is assumed that the terms are self-explanatory) it does speak to how reuse is to be paid, if at all, for each of these.

The first thing to point out is that the terms "promo" and "preview" would be considered the same and would fall into the terms of "trailers" or "promo." So, while promos or previews may be the same, recaps are not.

A recap is at the beginning of a given episode and is meant to bring the audience up to speed on the plot line of the given series to-date by showing clips from previous episodes. For this type of use, there actually would be reuse payments that may be due to a performer.

However, for something such as the scenario we have here, where the stunt performer's work from episode 18 is used at the end of episode 17 to promote or preview the next episode, that would fall into the producer's right to promote the episode the performance was taken from or the series as a whole, and such use can be done without having to pay for that reuse.

Key takeaway: Recaps = reuse pay may be owed. Promos/previews = generally no reuse pay (producer's promotional right).

Glossary

Key terms used in SAG-AFTRA contracts and on your Exhibit G, in plain language.

Network
ABC, CBS, or NBC. Network contracts have their own specific rate tables separate from Basic Cable or Theatrical agreements.
Basic Cable
Exhibition of TV programs via a cable system where no special program charge is made beyond the general cable subscription fee. Has its own SAG-AFTRA rate schedule.
Local Hire
You can work at the shooting location without production owing you travel expenses or per diem. You are responsible for getting yourself to set.
Regional Hire (Modified Local)
Production covers mileage (but not airfare) and sometimes lodging and per diem. You're still typically on a day rate contract.
Distant Location / Overnight Location
A shooting location outside the studio zone that requires an overnight stay. Travel time counts as work time; per diem is owed; rest periods are reduced only for Theatrical (not TV).
Per Diem
A daily meal allowance paid when on overnight location, only if meals are not provided by the Producer. SAG-AFTRA minimum: $12 + $18 + $30 = $60/day, paid in advance. Negotiated increases bring this to $70 (11/9/23) and $75 (11/9/25). Regardless of call time, the first major meal is deducted at the lunch rate only.
Exhibit G
Your SAG-AFTRA timecard. Documents your hours worked, contract type, stunt adjustments, meal breaks, and how your pay was calculated. Always request a copy.
Stunt Adjustment
Extra pay negotiated on top of scale for performing a specific stunt (car hit, high fall, fire gag, etc.). It appears on your Exhibit G. The adjustment must be factored into your base rate before overtime is calculated: OT hourly rate = (Scale Rate + Stunt Adjustment) / Straight-Time Hours. If accounting only divides the scale rate, every OT hour is underpaid. Example: $1,246 scale + $800 adjustment = $2,046 base. Hourly = $2,046 / 8 = $255.75/hr. OT at 1.5x = $383.63/hr. Check your Exhibit G: if the 1.5x and 2.0x rates look too low, the adjustment was likely excluded.
Studio Zone
A defined geographic area around a major studio. Work within the zone uses studio work rules; work outside triggers location work rules.
Forced Call
When production calls you back to work before your rest period has fully elapsed. Day performers: daily rate or $900, whichever is less. 3-day/weekly performers: one day's pay or $950, whichever is less.
Flat Deal (Stunt Coordinator)
A Stunt Coordinator contract type with its own minimum rate. Flat deal coordinators have NO overtime — they receive premium pay instead.
Location Allowance
An additional 4 hours of straight-time salary automatically owed to weekly performers on a 6-day overnight location workweek — whether those 6 days are worked or not. One of the most commonly missed payments.
Source: SAG-AFTRA Workweek
Non-Deductible (ND) Meal
A meal provided within 2 hours of your call time (at least 15 minutes, free of all activity). Does NOT count as your meal break — the 6-hour meal window still runs from your crew call time.
Loan-Out Corporation
A personal corporation through which a performer provides their services. Pay can be issued to the corp instead of the individual. Production must pay the correct entity per your contract.
Reuse
When footage of your performance is used again in a new context (recap episode, clip show, etc.), production owes you a reuse payment within 60 business days of exhibition.
Stunt Driving — 6 Qualifying Conditions
Driving qualifies as a stunt when ANY of these exist: (1) wheels leave driving surface, (2) tire traction broken, (3) driver's vision substantially impaired, (4) speed greater than normally safe for conditions, (5) aircraft in close proximity creating hazard, (6) high speed or close proximity of two or more vehicles creating danger.
Source: 2024 Jeffords Rules — SAG-AFTRA Hazardous Work
CW Supplement
The CW network has its own supplement/side agreement within the SAG-AFTRA framework. Some Nickelodeon productions also have special rate agreements.
Money Break
A rate threshold above which overtime is calculated on the money break amount rather than your full negotiated rate, effectively capping your OT rate. Day Performer (Theatrical & TV): 2x the minimum day rate. 3-Day Performer (TV): $3,000. Weekly/Series (TV): $4,400. Weekly/Series (Theatrical): $5,500.
Made for Pay TV
A program produced for pay television (e.g., premium cable/streaming), with scripted narrative and running time of approximately 75+ minutes. Has its own contract rules.
